Reading Response

The main affordances in my micro-project are the clickable dots, the ability to unlock all the dots to eventually get a star, and the airplane that moves with the cursor. The dots afford clicking because when you click them, they reveal a fact about that location. The system also affords progression, since clicking all the dots leads to unlocking something new. The airplane affords movement because it follows the user’s cursor across the screen.These affordances are signified in a few ways. The dots are signified as clickable through the instructions at the top of the screen that tell the user to click on locations. Also, once a dot is clicked, it turns gray, which shows that it has already been interacted with and helps communicate progress. Some affordances are less clear. The airplane follows the cursor, but this is not explained anywhere, although I feel that users will immediately realize they can control it. Also, the star that appears at the end is not signified at all, so users have no way of knowing there is a final outcome until they unlock everything. If someone used the system without instructions, they might still understand that the dots can be clicked because that is a common interaction, but they probably would not understand the full system or the goal. Since the system is literally a globe I feel like the mapping aspect is quite self explanatory. The relationship between clicking dots (input) and revealing facts (output) is intuitive, a user might hesitate at the end when everything is complete and wonder what is next? The main constraint is that only the dots can be clicked to unlock a fact, clicking on a location with no dot does not reveal a fact, this is not a clear constraint but it is intuitive. Norman may criticize that the design relies too much on discovery instead of making actions and outcomes obvious and there not being some greater goal being achieved rather than becoming a star.


Honestly, I loved this reading. It was so interesting to learn about human centric design through the lens of industrial design, but in relation to interaction and experience design. On a day to day basis, I tend to notice good design and bad design when I interact with the world, however, there are many things that I interact with that I neither notice good or bad design, which is the hallmark of good design because good design should be intuitive and natural. One of my favorite quotes from the text is “It is the duty of machines and those who design them to understand people, it is not our duty to understand the arbitrary, meaningless dictates of machines”. This quote is a good rule of thumb when designing, highlighting that we should design technology that caters to the user, not the other way around. As designers we should think logically and psychologically. An engineer only thinks logically, not fully understanding humans, leading to overly complex designs that don’t account for the inevitable errors that humans will make. However, it is a designer's job to think about human behavior to produce a seamless and pleasurable experience for the user. Another aspect of the reading that I found especially interesting and relatable is the idea that affordances and anti-affordances need to be perceivable, and how this brings up the issue with glass. In February, at work, I actually ran into a glass wall and cut open my eyebrow resulting in 6 stitches and a concussion. This is a great example of poor design, because they put a glass wall to the left of the exit of the bathroom. Almost all of my coworkers told me that they almost did the exact same thing multiple times, so this just shows it wasn’t just me, it was poor design.
